Introduction: Prerequisites & Goal Setting
Investment Prerequisites: Before investing, ensure three things:
Maximize employer pension contributions.
Clear all high-interest debt (investment returns are not guaranteed; debt interest is).
Build an Emergency Fund (highly liquid cash covering 3–12 months of essential living expenses) to prevent forced stock liquidations during market downturns.
Psychological Tolerance: Define your capacity for volatility. Long-term investors must be willing to endure 50% market drops without panic-selling.
Chapter 1: Market Fundamentals & Mechanics
Core Concepts
IPO (Initial Public Offering): The process of a company selling a percentage of its equity to the public to raise capital for expansion.
Secondary Offering: Issuing additional new shares after the IPO. Dilutes existing shareholders but raises new capital.
Stock Split: Dividing existing shares into multiple new shares (e.g., 2-for-1). Reduces the price per share without changing the company's total market cap or the investor's equity value. Increases liquidity.
Reverse Split: Consolidating shares (e.g., 1-for-2). Used when share prices fall dangerously low, risking delisting or creating a poor brand image.
Share Buybacks: The company repurchases its own shares from the open market. Reduces share supply, increasing the proportional ownership and Earnings Per Share (EPS) for remaining shareholders. Signals management believes the stock is undervalued.
Specific Examples
The Coconut Island (Market Origins): A man needs coconuts to pay workers to build a ladder to reach 90% of an island's coconuts. He partners with an investor who provides the initial coconuts in exchange for 50% of future profits. Both parties and the island's economy grow.
Limonada Ltd. (IPO Analogy): A fictional lemonade stand valued at 200M NIS. The founder wants to raise 100M NIS. He issues 10 million shares to the public at 10 NIS each, giving up 50% equity.
Nvidia (Stock Split): Used to illustrate a stock price growing so fast that retail investors are priced out, prompting a split to restore liquidity.
Chapter 2: Value Investing, Valuation & Financial Analysis
Core Concepts
Value Investing Premise (Buffett/Graham): "A good company is worth money." Long-term stock prices mirror the underlying cash-generation capability of the business.
Moat (Competitive Advantage): A structural advantage preventing competitors from stealing market share (e.g., brand loyalty, switching costs, monopolies).
Margin of Safety: Buying a stock at a significant discount (e.g., 2/3 of its intrinsic value) to protect against unforeseen risks or calculation errors.
Management Quality: Leadership with a proven track record of operational efficiency and shareholder alignment.
Unpolished Diamonds: Companies with massive underlying value requiring management changes or structural shifts to unlock their potential.
Macro vs. Micro Effects: Macro refers to global/national events (wars, interest rates, pandemics) that move the entire market. Micro refers to company-specific events (lawsuits, new products).
Cyclical vs. Seasonal Stocks: Cyclical stocks rise and fall with the broader economy (e.g., tourism). Seasonal stocks spike during specific times of the year.

Formulas & Ratios (With Course Targets)
Current Ratio: Current Assets / Current Liabilities. Target: 1.0 to 2.0. Measures ability to pay short-term obligations. >2.0 indicates inefficient use of cash.
Quick Ratio: (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities. Target: > 1.0. A stricter liquidity measure, as inventory (like winter coats) may be hard to sell quickly at full price.
Debt-to-Equity (D/E): Total Liabilities / Shareholders' Equity. Target: < 0.50. Ensures growth is driven by operations, not excessive leverage.
Operating Margin: Operating Profit / Revenue. Target: > 20%.
Revenue Growth: Target: > 10% YoY.
Net Profit Growth: Target: > 15% YoY.
FCF (Free Cash Flow) Growth: Target: > 10% YoY. FCF is the "oxygen" of the company, showing actual liquid cash available after operations and CapEx.
ROE (Return on Equity): Net Income / Shareholders' Equity. Target: > 15%. Measures how much profit the company generates from shareholder money.
ROIC (Return on Invested Capital): Operating Cash Flow / Invested Capital. Target: > 10%-15%. Measures management's efficiency in allocating capital to profitable investments.
EPS (Earnings Per Share): Net Income / Shares Outstanding.
P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings): Market Cap / Net Income (or Price / EPS). Represents how many years it takes the company to earn back its valuation. Historical healthy range is 10–20.
PEG Ratio: P/E Ratio / Expected Earnings Growth Rate. Used for Growth stocks. A lower PEG justifies a high P/E if growth is explosive.
Dividend Yield: Annual Dividend per Share / Stock Price. Target: Typically 1% to 3%.
Dividend Payout Ratio: Dividends Paid / Net Profit. Shows how much profit is returned vs. reinvested.
EBITDA: Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization. Course rule: Avoid using this. Buffett dislikes EBITDA because depreciation is a real business expense. Use FCF instead.
Investment Strategies Taught
Warren Buffett (Value): Focuses on predictable cash flows, high ROE/ROIC, clear moats, and holding "forever."
Peter Lynch (Growth/GARP): Focuses on companies in their aggressive growth phase. Accepts higher P/E ratios justified by extreme earnings growth. Requires deep niche understanding.
100-Baggers (Mayer/Phelps): Finding companies that multiply 100x by maintaining high ROIC and aggressive compounding over decades.
DCF (Discounted Cash Flow): Mathematical valuation predicting future cash flows. Best for predictable assets (e.g., gas reserves), heavily flawed for unpredictable tech growth.
Specific Examples
AutoZone: A masterclass in ROIC and Buybacks. ROIC grew from 12% (2000) to 33%. Heavy buybacks drastically reduced share count, causing the stock to soar despite boring overall revenue growth.
Apple & Microsoft: Examples of impenetrable moats and extreme ROE (Apple cited at 160% ROE).
Coca-Cola: Brand moat example. Beat the S&P 500 continuously for 35+ years simply because consumers refuse to buy generic substitutes.
Shufersal: Management quality example. Amir brothers took over; stock jumped 35% on the news alone, then 70% as they aggressively cut costs and pressured suppliers to boost Net Profit.
Starbucks & Chipotle: Brian Niccol generated a 7x return in 6 years at Chipotle. When announced as Starbucks' new CEO, SBUX jumped 15% in one day.
Scrub Daddy: "Unpolished Diamond" example. A good product turned into a $300M business only after receiving elite operational management via Shark Tank.
American Express (Salad Oil Scandal): Margin of Safety example. A scammer faked inventory using water topped with oil. AMEX stock crashed due to lawsuit fears. Buffett bought 5% of the company, knowing the core credit business was completely unharmed.
Burlington Northern Santa Fe (BNSF): Buffett bought this railroad in 2009 during a tech hype cycle. Traded at an 18 P/E. Boring, but irreplaceable infrastructure generating massive cash flow.
Dexter Shoe: Buffett's worst mistake. Paid $400M in Berkshire stock. The company was destroyed by cheap Chinese imports. Lesson: Moats must be future-proof; past performance doesn't guarantee survival against global macro shifts.
Six Flags vs. Spotify: Six Flags earnings graphs show extreme seasonality (summer spikes). Spotify shows non-seasonal, erratic growth. You must compare seasonal stocks YoY, not quarter-over-quarter.
Hyper Global: Example of calculating Adjusted ROE. On paper, profits dropped 23% YoY. However, removing a massive one-time Nvidia deal from the prior year revealed core operational profits actually grew 35%.
Chapters 3 & 4: Execution & Portfolio Management
Brokerage & Order Types
Brokerage: Open an account through a regulated investment house (Meitav Dash recommended: 15 NIS/month, min $5/trade for US stocks). Avoid wasting time constantly switching brokers.
Market Order: Executes immediately at the current market price. Risk: Slippage during high volatility.
Limit Order: Executes only at your specified maximum buy price or minimum sell price. Course recommendation: Always use Limit Orders to strictly control entry/exit prices, even if it means missing a trade.
Psychological Pitfalls & Portfolio Strategy
The Break-Even Fallacy: Refusing to sell a losing stock until it "gets back to what I paid." Rule: If the investment thesis is broken, cut losses immediately. Reallocate to better opportunities rather than waiting years to break even.
The Take-Profit Fallacy: Selling a stock just because it went up 20% to "lock in gains." Rule: Don't interrupt compound interest. Winners can keep winning. Selling triggers tax events and removes you from future upside.
Capital Allocation (New Deposits): When adding monthly savings to a portfolio, avoid blindly buying equal amounts (Fixed Ratio). Instead, allocate dynamically to "Strengthen Specific Stocks" that currently offer the best valuations or strongest updated thesis.
Portfolio Size: For beginners, hold 15 to 25 stocks. This provides statistical safety while maintaining a clear, manageable thesis for each holding.
The 4-Step Investment Framework
Finding a Winning Case (Ideation): Use screeners (free/paid tools) to identify companies with compelling narratives, clear business models, and future potential. Rely on expert analysis to narrow down the thousands of available stocks.
Checking Financial Health (Validation): Run the stock through financial data websites. Verify the company meets the strict numeric targets established in Chapter 2 (Current Ratio > 1, Debt/Equity < 0.50, ROE > 15%, consistent FCF/Revenue growth).
Tracking the Portfolio (Monitoring): Monitor quarterly reports and major macro events. You are looking only for fundamental shifts that break your long-term thesis, not reacting to short-term market noise.
Finding an Entry Point (Technical Analysis): Use charting tools as an optional final step to gauge market sentiment and pinpoint an optimized entry price for your Limit Order.
